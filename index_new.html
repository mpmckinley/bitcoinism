Analogy, Abstraction, and a Search for What Bitcoin Is

Bitcoin's radical novelty defies singular definition so we resort to endless metaphors, analogies, and abstractions in an attempt to describe it - not as a flaw, but as a feature of its uniqueness and a mirror of human sense-making. Bitcoinism isn’t only about exploring "what Bitcoin is," but a formulation of why we can't stop debating about what it is – it’s a story of human cognition clashing with radical innovation.

Bitcoin represents a novel cognitive event forcing us individually and collectively to update our conceptual maps of reality. In this recreation and redrawing of maps, we are discovering new beliefs and meaning. Some arriving faster than others, we have been and remain in a period of collective sensory motor loop formation in the realization of relevance.

Introduction

Another Bitcoin treatise, yay! If the supply of bitcoin is utterly finite, the supply of Bitcoin writing is quite the inverse. And I think this is a good thing.

In early 2009, a lone tree falling in the forest was heard by all, or at least a few. On the 3rd of January, the genesis blocked of Bitcoin was mined by Satoshi Nakamoto. A week later, the first open-source client was released. The rest as they say, is history. To summarize that history: the Bitcoin network and its associated units, bitcoin, have grown in every measure. What began as a cypherpunk project with little practical trajectory in which to succeed, has many times over proven a resilience and value-creating construct welcoming everyone from terrorists to multi-generational endowments into its folds.

As Bitcoin enters its second decade, passes various market capitalization milestones (and sometimes revisits them), …we must reflect. Reflect less on hash rate and market cap, less on the emergent Power Law characteristics themselves and its potential adherence to halving cycles. The most confounding thing about Bitcoin for many is not only that it is still here but seeing the continued evolution as to what it is in contrast to what it was.

About

Physics was built on a temporal assumption it could never test. From classical mechanics to quantum field theory, time was treated as a continuous parameter in the equations, a smooth backdrop against which particles move, fields evolve, and measurements occur. But any device we might use to measure time, any interaction, any particle, any signal, already unfolds inside that assumed temporal fabric. The clock and the thing being timed share the same medium. This makes the continuity of time a self-referential axiom rather than an experimentally falsifiable proposition: a system made of time cannot step outside time to examine its smallest unit, just as Gödel showed that a formal system cannot fully validate its own axioms from within.
